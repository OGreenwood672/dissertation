\section{System Performance}
\label{sec:4.2}

Reinforcement Learning with multiple agents requires millions of datapoints for updating the polcies and an equal number to evaluate the language and policy.
This section quantitivley measures the performance gains through the custom systems developed.
Specifically, it analyses the steps per second of the rust simulation developed and the memory efficiency of the \lstinline[language=Python]|RolloutBuffer|.
Benchmarks were evaludated on NVIDIA RTX 5060 Ti and AMD Ryzen 7 3700X.

\subsection{Vectorised Environment Throughput}
\label{sec:4.2.1}

A major issue with Python running multiple threads concurrently is the GIL.
While Python's builtin library \lstinline[language=Python]|multiprocessing| can, in theory, bypass this limitation, it introduces considerable overhead.
For this RL project, all threads require synchronisation at every simulation timestep.

To justify developing the simulation in Rust rather than Python, a simple mock simulation was developed in Python.
This mock simulation was compared to the vectorised Rust backend.
Environment steps per second (SPS) was calculated to analyse the throughput of each of the simulations.
Number of worlds parallised was scaled ($W \in \{2^0, 2^1, ..., 2^8\}$) to measure concurrency efficiency.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{sim-comparison.png}
    \caption{Concurrent Environment Throughput Performance. Comparison of Python multiprocessing and Rust with Rayon across an exponentially increasing number of parallel worlds}
    \label{fig:sim-time-comparison}
\end{figure}
% TODO: Test for how long?

As shown in figure \ref{fig:sim-time-comparison}, the Python baseline completely fails to match the Rust simulation.
Despite being able to bypass the GIL and using multiple CPU cores, the serialisation and the Inter-Process Communication overhead required to transfer the necassary data between isolated processes at every time step heavily bottleneck the throughput.

In contrast, the Rust is able utilise \lstinline[language=Rust]|Rayon|'s thread-pool to efficiently manage the parallel world steps nativly in C-space.
After a minor scheduling overhead at $W=2$, throughput scales linearly with number of worlds parallised in $\log_2$.
By using Rust's native Rayon threading, the sequential fraction of the program is minimised per Amdahl's Law allowing near-perfect scaling that saturates the CPU cores.

\subsection{Rollout Buffer Efficiency}

A secondary major potential bottleneck is transferring the results of the rollout phase to the training phase (across CPU-GPU boundary).
To evaluate the \lstinline[language=Python]|RolloutBuffer|, the memory allocation and transfer overhead is compared to a baseline method (appending to Python lists and executing \lstinline[language=Python]|torch.stack| after the rollout phase).

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
    \hline
    \textbf{Memory Strategy} & \textbf{Allocation Time (s)} & \textbf{Peak VRAM Overhead (MB)} \\ \hline
    \textbf{Dynamic Python Lists} & $6.47 \pm 0.18$ & $1213.45$ \\ \hline
    \textbf{Pre-allocated RolloutBuffer} & $6.79 \pm 0.23$ & $598.23$ \\ \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Memory Management Overhead per Epoch: $T=20,000$, $W=32$}
    \label{tab:rolloutbuffer-eval}
\end{table}

As shown in \ref{tab:rolloutbuffer-eval}, the \lstinline[language=Python]|RolloutBuffer| method reduces VRAM by over $50\%$.
This is as Python's dynamic arrays often require copying their contents from one memory location to another to increase their size.
Whilst there is a $\sim 5\%$ improvement in allocation time when using Python's dynamic list, the large improvement in VRAM will enable scaling this method to millions of datapoints.


\section{Offline Pre-training}
\label{sec:4.3}

Before we are able to integrate the language into reinforcement learning, its health must be evaluated.
A poorly initialised frozen encoder could limit the agents' language considerably, preventing them from reaching their maximum cooperation potential.

\subsection{Reconstruction and Commitment Quality}
\label{sec:4.3.1}

For this evaluation, reconstruction loss and commitment loss are tracked throughout a training process of 50 epochs using 26880 datapoints.
Liu et al. defined a healthy encoder for the AI Mother Tongue system.
Reconstruction loss should fall between 0.08 and 0.15.
Commitment Loss must remain below 1.0.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{minipage}{0.48\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{recon_loss_eval.png}
        \caption{Training and Validation Reconstruction Loss}
        \label{fig:recon_loss}
    \end{minipage}\hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.48\textwidth}
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{commit_loss_eval.png}
        \caption{Training and Validation Commitment Loss}
        \label{fig:commit_loss}
    \end{minipage}
    \caption{Convergence of tracked losses over 50 epochs (with K-means prior)}
    \label{fig:vae_training_eval}
\end{figure}

As shown in \ref{fig:vae_training_eval}, the model experiences rapid inital convergence.
By epoch 50, the reconstruction loss has converged to $\sim 0.065$.
Liu et al.'s recommended loss for a healthy codebook is based on low reconstruction is often a symptom of 'mode collapse'.
This is where the network memorises a small set of generalisable states.
However, as demonstrated in \ref{sec:4.3.2}, the model maintains a high utilisation of the latent space.
Therefore, this low loss indicates high effectiveness in the model rather than 'mode collapse'.

By the end of the training, the commitment loss has converged to $\sim 0.15$.
This is well below Liu et al.'s requirement.

\subsection{Initial Codebook Utilisation}
\label{sec:4.3.2}

A potential concern of using a VQ-VAE is the previously discussed 'mode collapse'.
The 'mode collapse' effectivly reduces the vocabulary size of the language restricting the expressiveness of the agents.
To evaluate this concern, an ablation study is performed.
Two initialisation strategies are compared:
a standard uniform random initalisation,
and a data-driven K-Means prior.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
    \hline
    \textbf{Initialisation Strategy} & \textbf{Final Validation Recon Loss} & \textbf{Active Codes} \\ \hline
    \textbf{Random Uniform Prior} & $0.064 \pm 0.01$ & $79.13 \pm 12.63$ \\ \hline
    \textbf{K-Means Prior} & $0.070 \pm 0.004$ & $83.53 \pm 7.67$ \\ \hline
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Memory Management Overhead per Epoch: $T=20,000$, $W=32$}
    \label{tab:init_vae_eval}
\end{table}

The table \ref{tab:init_vae_eval} shows the random uniform initialisation achieved a marginly lower reconstruction loss of $\sim 0.064$.
However, the training proved to be much more volatile than the K-Means initialisation method and analysis post-training revealed a average index utilisation.
This indicates that the VQ-VAE model initialised with random uniform embeddings is subject to the 'initialisation lottery', which can be mitigated by instead initialising codebook embeddings with a data-driven K-Means prior.
The reduced volatility of training the language ensures high reproducibility for MARL training.
